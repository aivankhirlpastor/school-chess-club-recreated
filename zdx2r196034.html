PROLOGUE TO CHESS

Chess is a 2-player strategy board game played on an 8x8 chequered board. Each player controls either the white or black pieces, and the goal is to trap the opponent's king in a position where it cannot move to safety. The chess board consists of 64 alternating colour squares arranged in an 8x8 grid.1 The game has a long history, originating in India around the 6th century AD and spreading to the Middle East and Europe.2

THE CHESS PIECE

One piece must be moved to a new square on each turn. The players alternately advance a piece after the person with the white pieces moves first. In chess, there are no dice; instead, each piece moves in a unique fashion, and you choose which piece to move. Plus, each piece can 'take', or capture, an opposing piece. All you have to do is move your piece or pawn onto the square that the opponent piece is on and take it off the board.3

ARRANGE THE SET

Every player must always have a light-colored square in their lower right corner of the board. Even TV shows occasionally make the error of putting the board in the incorrect orientation. You can begin adding the pieces as soon as the board is correctly oriented. On each player's side of the board, their pieces begin on the first rank (called "ranks" in chess) of horizontal rows.4

INTERPRETATION FOR THE PIECE MOVEMENT

  • PAWNS

    Move forward one square every turn, with the exception of their first turn, when they can advance two squares.

  • ROOKS

    Move in straight line: vertically or horizontally.

  • KINGHTS

    Move one square to the right or left of the second square after moving two squares either vertically or horizontally; knights jumps over the piece.

  • BISHOPS

    Move diagonally.

  • QUEENS

    Move in any course or direction.

  • KINGS

    Move one square in any course or direction.5


WINNING AT GAME

In the brief of the passages, winning at chess required demanding capabilities for the interpretation of the mechanics of the game.

  1. Think a few moves in advance and set up longer, more complicated attacks.
  2. Make good opening moves to set up the rest of the game.
  3. Do not give away pieces for free.
  4. Get your pieces in position.
  5. Coordinate an attack on the opponent's king.
  6. Watch the safety of your own king and always be a good sport.6
